De Bello Gallico Liber Primus:
An Annotated Edition

With Critical Apparatus, Geographic Notes, and Ethnographic Commentary
Vol. 156 · No. 1 · MMXXVI · pp. 1–32
DOI: 10.1234/tlpa.2026.001
Abstract

This edition presents the opening chapters (I.1–I.3) of Caesar's De Bello Gallico with a layered critical apparatus, geographic annotations, and ethnographic commentary situating the tripartition of Gaul within the wider discourse of Roman imperial ethnography. Drawing on the manuscript tradition (M, α, β, γ), the present text offers a fresh conspectus of the Gallia est omnis problem — the editorial question of whether the proemial sentence was authored by Caesar himself or by a later hand. The annotations follow the conventions of the TLPA style sheet and employ small-caps for all proper nouns in the original Latin, in conformity with classical-philological practice.

Keywords: Caesar · Gallia · Belgae · Ethnography · Latin Prose · Textual Criticism

Notae
  1. Cf. Cicero, De Officiis I.xi.36; Pliny, NH III.iv.33. On the tripartition, see Whitton (2019) 14–17; Riggsby (2006) 41–58. The reading Gallia est omnis is attested uniformly across the α and β families; the M MS omits est in certain late corrections. ↩
  2. On Caesar's compressed ethnographic style — three nouns, no verbs — see Norden (1939) 78 ff., who compares the parataxis with Thucydides I.3. ↩
  3. Garumna = Garonne; Matrona = Marne; Sequana = Seine. The triple is conventional; cf. Strabo IV.i.1. ↩
  4. On the ethnographic topoi of remoteness from cultus provinciae, cf. Poseidonius FGrHist 87 F 31; also Tacitus, Germania 5.3. ↩
  5. The parenthetic note on the Helvetii is conventionally regarded as an authorial aside rather than a scribal insertion: see Collins (1972) 12–14, contra Funaioli (1930). ↩
  6. Note Caesar's use of Oceanus as a quasi-spatial category rather than a proper noun: see Nicolet (1988) 113–28. ↩
  7. On septentriones and the construction of Celtic geography through Roman cardo-decumanus thinking, cf. Romer (1978) 88. ↩
  8. Corr.: Pyrenaei montes Pyrenaeos montes edd. The accusative is required by motion through; the nominative is lectio difficilior preserved in M. ↩
  9. Closing sentence of §I.1 has been variously suspected; the present edition follows Klotz (1927) in retaining it. ↩
