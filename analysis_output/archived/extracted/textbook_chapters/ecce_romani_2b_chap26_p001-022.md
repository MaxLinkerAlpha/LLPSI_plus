INTRODUCTION

"The adventures of the Cornelius family continue in Book IIB with Uncle Titus! visit
to the Baths of Nero. After school Eucleides takes Marcus and Sextus to the baths, where
Sextus distinguishes himself through a remarkable feat. The three then relax in the
library at the baths and listen to a poet reciting the story of Pyramus and Thisbe. Next,
Eucleides, the two boys, and Cornelia spend a rainy day at home playing children's
games. "The real games consisting of bouts between gladiators in the Amphitheater or
Colosseum are soon approaching, and Cornelius takes his son Marcus to see them.
Marcus reports back to Sextus, who is too young to go to the Amphitheater, and this is
followed by a telling of the famous story of Androcles and the lion. Cornelia, tired now
of her solitude in Rome, is suddenly summoned by her father into his study where she
hears the stunning announcement of a decision made by her father and mother. What
that is, you will find out as you read the stories in this book, but let it be said that it
marks a great transition in Cornelia' life. When the month of March arrives, Marcus
undergoes one of the great rites of passage for young Romans as he comes of age, puts
aside his toga preatexta and bulla, and puts on the toga of manhood. In the final Latin
story in the book, the family is about to return to Baiae to escape the heat of the city in
July, when Uncle Titus has a most unfortunate accident in the baths, from which he does
not recover. The sad event of his funeral is recounted. 'The subsequent lives of the
characters that you have come to know so well in the stories in ECCE ROMANI are
recounted in an epilogue, and the narrative portion of the book concludes with an essay
exploring the multicultural traditions within the historical and geographic scope of the
Roman Empire and tracing the multicultural influences of those traditions in subsequent
epochs of history, including our own.

"The stories in Book IIB thus allow you to experience some of the important cultural
pastimes of the Roman people and some of the key rites of passage that defined their
lives. 'The topics of the cultural essays include the baths, games played by children and
adults, the circus and arena, gladiators, opposition to the games, Roman weddings,
coming-of-age ceremonies, Roman religion, and Roman funerals. By reading the
passages from Latin authors in Tbe Romans Speak for Tbemselves that accompany Chapters
43, 49, and 53, you will communicate directly with the Romans and hear what they
themselves had to say about their baths, their gladiatorial shows, and their weddings.
You will also be able to communicate directly with the Romans by reading in the original
Latin the stories of Pyramus and Thisbe, Androcles and the lion, the Roman boy
Papirius Praetextatus, the stoic woman Arria, and the young woman Minicia Marcella,
who died shortly before her thirteenth birthday and just prior to her wedding. The Latin
texts of these stories are available in the Language Activity Book.

In Book IIB you will continue to follow the course of Roman history from the early
empire to the age of Constantine and on to the fall of the Roman Empire. You will
continue your study of word building in Latin and in English derivatives, and you will
see some of the connections between Latin and the Romance languages.

AT THE BATHS

Oz of the main entertainments of the Romans was their daily visit to the baths —
either to the public thermae or to the smaller, private balneae. They would expect to find
three basic rooms: a warm room (tepidárium), which one would enter after undressing in
the changing-room (apodyterium); a hot room (caldarium), where hot water would be
provided in a specially heated room, which might also incorporate a steam bath; and
a cold room (frigidarium), where one could plunge into a cold bath after the heat of
the caldarium.

In the big public bathing establishments one would also expect to find an exercise
ground (palaestra), often in the open air, with a covered portico around it, where one
might engage in a variety of exercises. There were various types of ball games, including a
game involving the "snatching" (rapere) of a heavy ball (harpastum) and trigon, a
throwing and catching game played by three people with a ball also called trigon, from the
name of the game. Men might engage in wrestling (lüctari), fencing at a post (palus), or
weightlifting. After exercising, the Roman would be rubbed down with oil (unguentum),
which cleansed and refreshed the skin. The excess oil was then removed with a special
metal instrument (strigilis). Then the skin would be rubbed down with a towel (linteum).

In Rome the baths opened at noon and remained open till dusk. 'T'he opening and
closing times were indicated by the striking of a gong.

Many establishments had separate facilities for men and women bathers; others fixed
different hours for the two sexes.

"The baths were regarded as social clubs, and people went there to exercise, play
games, meet each other, and share gossip and news, as well as to bathe.
